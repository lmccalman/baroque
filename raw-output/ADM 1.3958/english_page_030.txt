Paris, January 18, 1771.

The Court is still agitated by new intrigues, and it is
well known that the Duke of Praslin's skill has not brought Peace to it.

It has been learned that the Chancellor and the Duke d'Aiguillon
quarrel mortally, and mutually seek to supplant each other.
They should even have had a very heated quarrel, in which the
Duke must have reproached the Chancellor for his deceit, his maneuvers
[fuy à quoi] the other replied, [ne pourlés point], and remember,
you that it is [par davan], but henceforth [voud faire faire] your trial
when I wish. [Un y esiste] even that [il le cette menare], it
might well be a question in Parliament of examining the Duke's affair.

The Chancellor has another adversary in the Abbé Terray,
who [va quelque les brasses], and [peutne par laz] at Court, seems to
seek to raise himself on his ruins. The Abbé [sans l'étenduë d'asylé]
by the [Parties de Genêt], who are today the pillage and the executioner,
of which he is the former [corestan], having been [Repecteur] General.

The Prince of Condé has not entered the Council as
it had been said, but [je renvoi uniquement] for a given point of
precedence to the other Princes of the Blood, of which three have again
Duke of Orleans, but he has taken no less influence.

[On vient d'assujettir], and one still universally detests
the manipulations and [renoits] of Parliament [redicairement]
