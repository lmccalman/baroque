SPEECH
By Monsieur THE CHANCELLOR,
to Parliament.

GENTLEMEN,

HIS MAJESTY could have limited his views to repairing the losses
of the Magistracy.

But his tenderness for his people has fixed his gaze on the ad-
ministration of justice, & it is of greater utility,
that wisdom will bring forth a happier order & long
desired by our fathers.

Venality introduced by the necessity of circumstances,
seems to debase the most august ministry, by making one purchase the
right to exercise it. It removes from the Prince's choice what is most
flattering about it, & steals from merit a part of its reward
by admitting fortune to share in it.

The Magistrate who devotes himself to the most painful labors,
still fears that this very devotion may be slandered, &
that in paying homage to the utility of his services, one may not
render it to the purity of his views.

In the extent of an immense jurisdiction, his zeal finds
ever-renewing occupations, but always the impossibility
of discharging his debt & of relieving all the unfortunate who have
claims on his ministry.
