From a Manuscript, entitled: The Maxims of R. de P. 3e. Mainthe.

ON JUSTICE

WHEN I wished to cast my eyes upon all the tribunals of my Kingdom, I found an army of Legal professionals all deemed honest people, but all suspected of not being so. Each tribunal had a superior; I myself had mine; for opposition was formed to the judgments rendered by my Council, & I did not get angry about it; because I was a custom. In examining the progress that justice was making in my States, I was alarmed to see that in a century, the tenth part of my subjects had enlisted under the banners, & in calculating what was needed to provide for these legions, I trembled, when I saw that the tenth part of the revenues of my kingdom would pass through their hands.

But what gave me the most uneasiness, was this sure & constant course that men of law have, this spirit of liberty inseparable from their principles & this skillful way of preserving their advantages, & of crushing their enemies by the appearances of the most exact equity. I reviewed in my memory all those vigorous acts of the Parliaments of England, & of Paris, & if I often admired, I was sometimes quite ashamed for the majesty of the throne.

It is in the midst of all these reflections that I determined to undermine the foundations of this great power, & it is only by simplifying it as much as I could, that I reduced it to the point where I desired it.
