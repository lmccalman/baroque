Private Letter

Paris, April 29, 1775.

The Protestations
of the Princes dated the 4th of April beginning
to spread in manuscript, have made the greatest
effect.
The Chancellor always active, always
intriguing, is presently occupied
in having negotiations conducted with the Princes;
it is assured that the Duke of Penthièvre is
charged with this Commission, and it is
even said that he has arrived at the Tuileries,
that one had formal protestations
from the peers, [peut changer de les tenir];
it is necessary however that [le qu'on l'en fleuve]
at the Court, to persist in a
Revolution, which absolutely cannot
be accomplished, or at least subsist without
the [Conveau].
One knows no less here that of the
Dukes and Peers, and the spirit of the court,
of the Court, and which was not unanimous,
seem more enough to extend.
One has not yet sent to the Châtelet
the Edict of Suppression of the old Parliament
and of Creation of the new one; the Chancellor
