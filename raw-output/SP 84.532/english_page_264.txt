granting our authority, in the hope that reflection
would bring them back to their duty; but our kindness itself has served
only to encourage their resistance, and to multiply unjust
acts, which have finally left us only the alternative either of
punishing them, or of sacrificing the most essential rights of our
Crown. Obliged to give Judges to our subjects, we
had first had recourse to the Officers of our Council; whose
talents, enlightenment, zeal and services have always
merited our confidence; but after having provided for the need of the
moment, we have cast our gaze further, and we have
felt that the interest of our peoples, the good of justice and our
glory itself solicited, in these circumstances, the reform
of abuses in the administration of justice: We have recognized
that the venality of Offices, introduced by the misfortune of the times,
was an obstacle to the choice of our Officers, and often kept away
from the Magistracy those who were most worthy of it by their
talents and by their merit: That we owed to our subjects a
justice prompt, pure and free; and that the slightest mixture
of interest could only offend the delicacy of Magistrates
charged with maintaining the inviolable rights of honor and
property: That the excessive extent of the jurisdiction of our
Parliament of Paris was infinitely harmful to litigants,
obliged to abandon their families to come solicit a
justice slow and costly: That already exhausted by the expenses of
voyages and displacements, the length and multiplicity of
proceedings completed their ruin, and forced them
often to sacrifice the most legitimate claims: Finally we
have considered that the custom which subjects the Lords to the costs
entailed by the prosecution of crimes committed within the extent of
their justices, was for them a very heavy burden, and
sometimes a motive to favor impunity. Consequently
