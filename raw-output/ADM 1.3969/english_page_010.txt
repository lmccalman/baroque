Privateer at the beginning of the war, he had been
taken by a French Frigate, & that, because he had
the manners & language of a Frenchman, he had been kept
several months in the dungeon. Consequently, he said, it is
just to do the same to all those who have such manners.

As he conversed with me thus, we were surrounded by a
swarm of Privateers of the same kind; three or four sent
boats on board, & wanted, they said,
to share the prize. Comparing these sorts of Frenchmen to some
that I had seen in Granada, I could not defend
myself from a certain movement of indignation against a
part of humanity. The idea of being always treated as an
enemy, by people who spoke the language of my country,
had something afflicting about it which weighed upon my Heart.

After three entire days of crossing, & similar visits
during which the rest of the provisions
I had embarked were consumed. The unfortunate Holland cast
anchor at Mevagisley. I was quite alarmed to see a large Ship anchor
in a place where there was only a Port
for Fishermen, & on a coast bordered by dreadful
& menacing rocks; but I did not delay in understanding that
these Privateers have an interest in bringing their prizes to places
removed from the eyes of Justice & the Admiralty of
England, & where they are entirely Masters. Such is the
Village of Mevagisley. As soon as we were near the coast,
