The Regulations on wheat, wines, police and others of those of most of the States, of the Government should also be printed, they interest the public which must conform to them; and above all the Citizens and Bourgeois, who always have some part in the government, and who are by their [d√©mission] or commerce members of the State. They are necessarily bound to the State, and Sovereign, to make order, justice, abundance reign there, and to prevent the Monopolies which reign, everywhere; reign too unfortunately in our City. There were in 1730 military Regulations made by the Small Council; they should be militarily printed and known to everyone; since they must doubtless regulate the time of exercises, the inspection of arms; the number of Officers of the Corps and their rank; that of the regiments and Companies, and their parade ground; the order and discipline of the bourgeois troops, their equipment or clothing, the service to which they are obliged; and the posts which they must occupy on occasions. The Edict of 1711, ordered all Merchants to have their partnerships registered in Chancellery; for this there is in the Chancellery a register where all partnerships are recorded, and signed by the Merchants who formed them; and the registration right is a Regulation on commerce, very judicious and very extensive; which must be from approximately the beginning of the century; this Regulation is of interest to Merchants and is very useful to them, why then is it not printed, and a Copy delivered to each of them, when they establish themselves or form their partnerships?

It is said that there is a Regulation of the Two-Hundred, which states that the Small Council cannot make an extraordinary expenditure that goes to six thousand florins, without being brought to the Two-Hundred, to be authorized; and another which states that if individuals believe themselves injured in their rights, and have some subject of complaint against the Small Council, they can have recourse to the Two-Hundred, to have it decided; whether these two Regulations exist, and if they have been observed.

The Regulations of the Two-Hundred belong to the Two-Hundred Council, and should therefore be printed and known to the Members; by means of a Copy which would be delivered to each; each would know his rights and attributions to the Small Council superior to the Council as well as to the Body of the [forte] the Registry of the Nation, and in general thus between the Magistrate and the people; instead, a State in a position to maintain its prerogatives, must prevent the usurpations that one would want to carry to [derner certifier] the conformities of Regulations prevent the Two-Hundred from making other propositions which are often contrary to the duties of the time.
