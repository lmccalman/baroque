A permanent Senate, always
assembled, always composed of the
same members, & which has particular interests separated from those
of the people, a Senate already vested with
that power so terrible among men, to pronounce sovereignly
on their fortune, on their life & on
their honor, should it participate in
legislation? Would this not introduce into the State the despotism
most dangerous of all? There is
no liberty, says Montesquieu,

                                (11)
when the power to judge is not
separated from the legislative power...
If they are joined, the power over the
life & liberty of citizens would be
arbitrary, for the judge would be legislator.

This frightening reunion of powers is found, in effect, only
among despots & in Aristocracies. It was the scourge of peoples under our old feudal government. The Lords sent
to the Provinces with a removable power to exercise sovereign rights there in the name of the King, had, taking advantage of the weakness of the government to erect their offices into
hereditary patrimonies. At the beginning of the 3rd Race, they
owed the Monarch nothing more than a seigneurial right. The Barons received, or did not receive the ordinances of our Kings, according as they
appeared to suit them, or not, under the [Barule Lege]. However, being at once judges & legislators, servitude necessarily became the lot of the people. Then one counted
