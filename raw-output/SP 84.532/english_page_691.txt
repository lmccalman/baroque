DECREE
OF THE COUNCIL OF STATE OF THE KING,
Of June 12, 1771.

Extract from the Registers of the Council of State.

THE KING having had represented to him a Decree rendered by his Parliament of Toulouse on March 18, and a Resolution made by said Parliament on May 24 last; by which Decree, while stating that very humble remonstrances would be made to His Majesty on the Letters Patent of January 23 last, and on his Edict of the following month of February, protesting that the rights of its jurisdiction, that one had never found in said Parliament anything but prompt and pure justice: [Ey recevroit] gratuitous when it shall please His Majesty to order it, declared that the Laws of the kingdom never permit it to recognize as Parliament and Superior Councils, the Judges established by said Letters Patent and Edict; nor to grant any authority or authenticity to the acts which had emanated, or which could emanate, from them; consequently made prohibitions, both to the Officers of the Chancellery near said Parliament, and to all Bailiffs, Seneschals and other Judges of the jurisdiction to seal and deliver any [pareatis] on said acts, or to permit their execution; nor to have any regard to the judgment, or to any commission which would be addressed to them by reason of the same; as also to all Bailiffs, Sergeants or Bailiffs, to make any writs tending to bring the inhabitants of the jurisdiction before said Judges: And under penalty of suspension or other arbitrary penalty: Made likewise prohibitions to all subjects of the King, being within the jurisdiction, of whatever quality and condition they may be, to summon, plead and defend before said Judges, under the penalties of law; declaring that it will not cease to beseech His Majesty to order the recall and reestablishment of the Parliament of Paris, to render to its authority the most favorable defenders, and the most attentive to the Princes of his Blood and to the
